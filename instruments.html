BODHRÀN
The low sounding drum that is sometimes capable of descending into the sepulchral regions of depth itself, such a vital ingredient in the performance of traditional Irish folk music. It is said that in order for a true bodhràn to sound as it should, it ought to “sound like thunder”. It is used sparsely on “Still A Mystery To Me” but its presence can still be felt.
NAAL
A two-headed drum from Nepal that was built in different sizes. We use a small one on “Telling Those Lies” but we also have its big brother which is almost the size of a conga.
SHRUTI BOX
Another drone instrument from India, it is built on the harmonica/accordion principle of free-beating reeds and blends in beautifully with the bowed double-bass and other low D drones on the “Still A Mystery To Me” intro. The word ‘shruti’ means ‘micro (musical) interval’ in Hindi.
A whole range of musical instruments has been used in the recording of the songs on this album and we should like to outline some of the details of the more obscure ones, those that are either exotic or vintage or both, their musical characteristics, their history…
BALALAÏKA
The balalaika is the national instrument of Russia and, with its three metal strings, (two of which are tuned in unison), triangular body shape and tiny sound hole, sounds like no other plucked stringed instrument. You can hear it towards the end of “Make-Believe” in company with acoustic & 12-string guitars and Dobro mandolin.
BANJO
Russ plays a nylon-strung banjo on “Some Lonely Night”, an S.S. Stewart, a company that predominantly made banjos but also manufactured mandolins, and this one dates from the 1880s which places it more in the antique rather than vintage category.
UKULELE-BANJO
The one we use on the album is a pre-1930 Gibson with one unusual distinguishing feature : a ‘trap door’ mechanism involving being able to open half of the back section, (which is divided into two parts), thus altering the tone-colour and increasing volume, very handy in concert but we kept it closed on the recording.
UKULELE
The date usually quoted for the invention of this instrument by Portugese immigrants in Hawaii, (with which it is inextricably associated), is 1879. To put it fully into perspective, this was only a few years before the banjo used on this album was made! The one I use on this record is a beautiful tenor instrument made from koa, (the wood traditionally used to make ukuleles, indigenous to Hawaii). and comes from Russ’ impressive collection of ukes. The maker’s name is Pono for whom it was constructed by the Ko’olau Guitar & Ukulele Company, “handcrafted on the Island of Java”.
NEAPOLITAN MANDOLIN
The instrument that Myles plays on “No Song To Sing” is a circa 1920 mandolin that was made by the Stridente company in Naples, thus providing total authenticity!
ELECTRIC TAMBURA (or tanpura)
The original acoustic tambura is a fretless 4-string instrument with a very long neck, played seated, usually by a lady. Its chief role is as an obligato drone accompaniment to the sitar. In fact, the sitar itself originally only had three strings, hence its name : si’tar in Hindi literally means ‘three strings’. The electric version is a hybrid box-shaped device that allows for precision tuning and is the perfect foil for the other ‘drone’ instruments on the introduction to “Still A Mystery To Me”. Russ found it in India.
LAP STEEL
The lap steel or Hawaiian guitar is played, as its name suggests, on the knees by sliding a solid metal bar, (as opposed to a hollow bottleneck), across its strings. This model is a German-made Hofner and dates from around 1950. Steve uses it on 3 tracks on the album and plays it with great feeling.
WASHBOARD TIE
A kind of metal washboard with an undulated surface in the shape of a tie that is slipped conveniently into the neck of a shirt or sweater and is traditionally played by scraping along its length with thimbles attached to the player’s fingers. On this recording, Russ uses a teaspoon to good effect. This instrument was a gift from a friend who bought it in New Orleans.
CUICA
A Brazilian friction drum made in California by the Gonbops company. It is played by inserting the hand into the drum and rubbing on a stick (which itself is inserted into the centre of the skin of the drum), between the thumb and index finger with the aid of a damp sponge or a piece of leather in order to make the skin vibrate, thus producing the sound. The cuica plays an important role in traditional Brazilian folk music and its inimitable sound can be heard on “Teenage Tears”.
TRIANGLE
This one was custom built by the eminent French percussion maker, Daniel Frouvelle, who lives and works in the Pyrenees. It was created after mediaeval and Renaissance iconographical evidence and, most notably, from Marin Mersenne’s famous treatise, Harmonie Universelle, of 1636. It differs from the modern orchestral triangle in that it is made of solid brass and has several rings, similar to curtain rings, which jingle ‘in sympathy’ to the basic sound, (a ‘deader’ sound), and provide an added, lighter timbre, rich in harmonics. On this record, it is played alongside the cuica on “Teenage Tears”.
